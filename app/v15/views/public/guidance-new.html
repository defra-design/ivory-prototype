Guidance

How to legally sell or buy ivory

Published 2 December 2019
From:
Animal and Plant Health Agency

Ivory items you can legally sell or buy

The Ivory Act 2018 makes it illegal to deal in most items that contain elephant ivory. There are some exemptions.

! Warning You can be fined up to £250,000 and sent to prison if you sell, buy or otherwise deal in elephant ivory that does not meet the exemption criteria.

Items that are exempt from the ivory ban

The following items can be bought, sold or hired, but only if the owner tells us about them first:

  • musical instruments made before 1975 with less than 20% ivory by volume
  • items made before 1947 with less than 10% ivory by volume
  • portrait miniatures made before 1918 with a surface area smaller than 320 square centimetres
  • items that a qualifying museum intends to buy or hire
  • items made before 1918 that are rare and extremely valuable

Tell us you want to sell or hire out an exempt item using our online service.

If you’re not completely certain if your item is exempt, the service can help you decide.

What you can do with ivory that is not exempt

If you own ivory that does not meet the Ivory Act exemption criteria, you are legally allowed to:

  • keep it
  • give it away as a gift
  • give it to someone in your will

How the Ivory Act helps protect elephants

The government believes that demand for ivory will exist so long as there are legal markets for it.

Legal markets make it easier for people to sell new ivory from recently killed elephants. For example, illegal ivory traffickers use artificial staining and other ageing techniques, which make it extremely difficult for anyone to tell the difference between illegal and legal ivory.

Closing down domestic ivory markets will reduce the demand for ivory, which in turn will help save elephants in the wild.

The UK’s ivory ban is one of the toughest in the world. It aims to:

  • remove the financial value from ivory
  • reduce the opportunity for new ivory to be laundered through legal markets
  • significantly reduce the flow of ivory from the UK to overseas markets
  • send a clear message to the rest of the world that the UK no longer considers ivory to be an acceptable commodity
  • encourage other countries to take similar actions

Check if your item meets the exemption criteria

The ivory ban extends to most items that contain elephant ivory.

How to tell if an item contains elephant ivory

It can be extremely difficult to tell the difference between:

  • elephant ivory
  • other types of ivory, such as walrus
  • fake ivory, such as ivorine
  • certain types of animal bone

If you are not sure about the item you want to sell or buy, you must consult an expert first.

Items that contain replacement ivory

You cannot sell or buy an exempt item if it’s been repaired with ivory taken from an elephant on or after 1 January 1975.

Guidance for musical instruments

A musical instrument (or accessory used to play a musical instrument) is exempt from the ivory ban if it:

  • was made before 1 January 1975
  • has less than 20% ivory by volume

You must tell us before you sell or hire one out.

You’ll need to treat accessories used to play a musical instrument, for example, a violin bow, as separate items when you tell us about them.

What is not a musical instrument

You cannot register an item as a musical instrument if it is:

  • in the shape of a musical instrument but not designed to be played
  • used to create music or rhythm but was not made originally for that purpose
  • an individual piano key or a set of keys

When you don’t need to register a musical instrument

You do not need to register an instrument to:

  • play it to an audience, even if you’ll be paid
  • teach lessons
  • take it abroad for personal use, professional performance or repair

How to measure the ivory volume of musical instruments

Make a reasonable assessment of the volume of ivory by either:

  • estimating by eye, if it’s easy to tell
  • calculating the amount of ivory as a percentage of the total volume of the material the item is made of

Don’t include empty spaces within an item in your measurement, for instance the space within a violin or piano. Any bag, for example in a set of bagpipes, should be deflated when making the assessment.

If it is difficult to know whether an item has empty spaces, you don’t need to damage it to find out; you can assess it based on knowledge of similar items.

Items with less than 10% ivory

Items with less than 10% ivory by volume are exempt from the ivory ban if:

  • they were made before 3 March 1947
  • the ivory in the item is integral to the item

You must tell us before you sell or hire one out.

The ivory must be integral

This means that one of the following must be true:

  • the ivory is essential to the design or function of the item
  • you cannot remove the ivory easily or without risk of damage

Examples of items where the ivory is integral include:

  • a detachable ivory knob from a teapot or measuring instrument
  • an original ivory inlay on a piece of furniture

An ivory statue which has been temporarily mounted on a plinth would not qualify.

How to measure ivory volume of items with less than 10% ivory

Make a reasonable assessment of the volume of ivory by either:

  • estimating by eye, if it’s easy to tell
  • calculating the amount of ivory as a percentage of the total volume of the material the item is made of

Don’t include empty spaces within an item when calculating the total volume of the item. For example, the space within a chest of drawers or a teapot does not count towards its total volume.

If it is difficult to know whether an item has empty spaces, you don’t need to damage it to find out; you can assess it based on knowledge of similar items.

For some items with thin pieces of ivory on the surface (inlays), it may be difficult to calculate the depth of the ivory. The surface area of the ivory inlay may be over 10% of the total surface area of the item, but the ivory volume may still be below 10%. This is because the inlay is so thin.

Portrait miniatures

A portrait miniature is a portrait usually created in the 18th or 19th century, often painted on very thin pieces of ivory.

Portrait miniatures are exempt from the ivory ban if:

  • they were made before 1 January 1918
  • the surface area you can see is less than 320 square centimetres

You must tell us before you sell or hire one out.

How to measure a portrait miniature

The surface area you can see must be less than 320 square centimetres. This does not include the frame or areas covered by the frame.

Selling or hiring to qualifying museums

Ivory items you intend to to sell or hire out to a qualifying museum are exempt from the ivory ban. The item does not need to meet any other exemption criteria.

You must tell us before you sell or hire one out.

A qualifying museum is one that is accredited in the UK by, or on behalf of:

  • Arts Council England
  • Museum Galleries Scotland
  • Northern Ireland Museums Council
  • The Museums, Archives and Libraries Division of the Welsh Government
  • the International Council of Museums (ICOM)

So long as the item is not raw (‘unworked’) ivory, a qualifying museum does not need to tell us if they buy, sell or hire ivory items to another qualifying museum. However, if they buy or sell an ivory item to an ICOM member, the export or import of ivory must comply with EU Wildlife Trade Regulations.

All museums (qualifying or not) can continue to accept donations and gifts of ivory items, as long as they are not in return for any other goods or services.

How to sell or hire out an ivory item

If your item is exempt from the ivory ban, you must tell us before you:

  • sell or hire it out
  • offer to sell or hire it out
  • exchange it for goods or services
  • keep it for sale or hire
  • export or import it with the intention to sell or hire it out

Rare and extremely valuable items

If your item is rare and extremely valuable, you’ll need to apply for an expert to certify the item. You must wait to receive your exemption certificate before you can sell or hire out the item. This usually takes 30 days.

Other exempt items

For all other items that are exempt from the ivory ban, you’ll need to complete an online self-assessment. After this, you’ll be able to sell or hire out your item immediately.

If you self-assess an ivory item, you must be certain that it meets all the exemption criteria. If we later find out that it does not, we may seize the item.

Tell us you want to sell or hire out an exempt item.

When you may also need to apply for a CITES permit or license

As part of the Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora (CITES), you may also need to buy a permit or certificate to trade an item made from an endangered species.

If you intend to:

  • import or export elephant ivory that meets the Ivory Act exemption criteria, you’ll also need to apply for a CITES permit
  • sell or hire out a musical instrument made between 1947 and 1975 that meets the Ivory Act exemption criteria, you’ll also need to apply for a CITES certificate

What to do when you buy an ivory item

If you are buying or hiring an ivory item from someone else, you must be certain that the item is exempt before you proceed.

We can seize items that we later find out were traded illegally. We can also fine or prosecute anyone who buys or hires illegal ivory if we discover that they did not do everything possible to comply with the law.
